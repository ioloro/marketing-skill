# Marketing Copy Skill

## Modes

This skill operates in four modes. Detect which mode the user wants from context. If ambiguous, ask: "Are we reviewing existing copy, building a PR Kit, planning an App Preview video, or planning App Screenshots?"

- **Copy Review** — review and improve existing marketing copy, section by section
- **PR Kit** — generate a complete press kit from scratch, component by component
- **App Preview** — plan an App Store preview video (15-30s) beat by beat
- **App Screenshots** — plan App Store screenshots (up to 10 per device per locale)

All four modes share the same upfront configuration gathering. App Preview and App Screenshots also capture device targets and locale strategy.

---

## Shared Configuration

Before starting either mode, capture from the user:

### Brand Rules
- Words or phrases to always use (e.g., exact product name)
- Words or phrases to never use (e.g., "just," "simply," "easy")
- Any intentional exceptions and why

### Product Facts
- What is actually available at launch vs. planned
- Accurate feature-to-tier mapping for pricing
- Geographic availability
- Any stats that appear in copy and their source
- Platform(s) and OS compatibility

### Voice
- 2-3 adjectives that describe the brand tone
- A "this sounds like us / this doesn't" example if available
- The product's core positioning in one sentence

### PR Kit Only (additional config)
- Media contact: name, email, and optionally phone
- Target audience for the kit: consumer press, tech press, trade/B2B press, podcasters, etc.
- Any existing press coverage to reference
- Whether this is a launch kit, funding announcement, or evergreen kit

## Copy Review Mode

### Overview

Facilitates a structured three-agent marketing review for product copy. Works section by section, proposes changes, gets approval, then writes. Never edits copy without first discussing it with the panel.

Before starting, also ask for:
- **Sections:** List the sections on the page or in the material, in order. Work through them one at a time.

### The Copy Review Panel

Every copy decision runs through three agents before a word is changed:

**GTM (Go-to-Market):** Accuracy, positioning, and conversion. Flags false claims, misrepresented features, premature CTAs, and availability overstatements. Asks the hard questions about what is actually true right now.

**CMO (Chief Marketing Officer):** Brand voice and section-level strategy. Ensures copy is positioned strongly, sounds like the product, and protects the emotional arc of the page. Pushes for stronger statements and sharper differentiators.

**Copywriter:** Word-level craft. Kills weak closers, fixes rhythm, enforces the grammar rules, and rewrites anything that tells readers how to feel instead of showing them what they get.

### Universal Copy Rules (Copy Review)

These apply to every project unless the user explicitly overrides them:

1. **No em dashes in sentences.** Replace with a period. "X — Y" becomes "X. Y." It's almost always stronger.
2. **No unverified stats.** Flag any number before publishing. Ask the user to confirm or remove it.
3. **No CTAs that create false expectations.** If the action isn't available yet (app not launched, feature not built), remove the CTA or redirect to a waitlist.
4. **Lead with benefit, not mechanism.** Users care what it does for them, not how it does it.
5. **Don't tell readers how to feel.** Never write "beautifully designed" or "presented cleanly." Show what they get.
6. **End on the strong word.** Don't trail off with qualifiers. Cut the last weak clause.
7. **Frame negatives positively.** "Works offline" not "doesn't need signal." "Refreshed monthly" not "expires monthly."
8. **Short punches over long sentences.** Three short sentences beat one compound sentence. Rhythm matters.

### Copy Review Process

1. **Read the section** before proposing any changes.
2. **Run the panel.** Present the current copy. GTM, CMO, and Copywriter each respond. Synthesize into a concrete proposal.
3. **Propose before writing.** Show the proposed copy and get explicit approval. Never edit without approval.
4. **Flag accuracy issues immediately.** If copy makes a claim that seems unverified or outdated, pause and ask before proceeding.
5. **Go section by section.** Don't jump ahead. When the user says "next," move to the next section.
6. **One change at a time for visual or structural elements.** Describe the change and get approval before touching code or layout.

### What the Copy Review Panel Looks For

**GTM looks for:**
- Claims that don't match the actual product
- Features listed at the wrong tier
- Geographic or availability overstatements
- CTAs pointing to actions that aren't yet possible
- Stats that need verification

**CMO looks for:**
- Off-brand tone or vocabulary
- Headers that describe instead of position
- Sections that undersell or oversell relative to the product's actual differentiation
- Emotional arc across the full page

**Copywriter looks for:**
- Weak closers at the end of sentences or sections
- Em dashes where a period would hit harder
- Passive or vague language ("reads your round through your body")
- "Just," "simply," "easy," or any word that minimizes the product
- Telling readers how to feel instead of what they get
- Any forbidden words from the brand rules

### Common Patterns to Catch

- Product name inconsistency — confirm the exact brand name and enforce it everywhere
- Overloaded feature bullets — each bullet should be one clear thing
- Pricing copy that doesn't match the app's actual tier gating
- Stats without confirmed sources
- "Worldwide" or similar scope claims that aren't accurate at launch
- Qualifier creep — "basic," "simple," "easy" softening strong features
- CTAs on pre-launch pages pointing to unavailable actions

## PR Kit Mode

### Overview

Generates a complete press kit from scratch, one component at a time. Unlike Copy Review, this mode is generative — there's no existing draft to review. The panel proposes each component, gets approval, then finalizes it. Never move to the next component without explicit sign-off.

The goal: a journalist should be able to cover the product accurately without asking a single follow-up question.

### The PR Kit Panel

**GTM (Go-to-Market):** Newsworthiness and accuracy. Would a journalist find this worth covering? Every claim must be true and verifiable right now. Flags anything that overstates availability, scale, or impact. Ensures stats are confirmed before they appear.

**CMO (Chief Marketing Officer):** Positioning and brand voice. Does this represent the brand at its strongest? Is the story compelling to the target press audience (consumer, tech, trade)? Pushes for sharper differentiators and a consistent narrative thread across all components.

**PR Director:** Press-specific craft. Knows what journalists actually need and how they use kit materials. Writes boilerplate that's tight enough to paste directly. Keeps bios credible without being braggy. Ensures the fact sheet is scannable in 30 seconds. Catches anything that sounds like marketing copy when it should sound like a press document.

### PR Kit Components (in order)

Work through these one at a time. Propose, get approval, finalize, then move on.

1. **Company Boilerplate** — 2-3 sentences, press-ready. This is what gets pasted at the end of articles and into press releases. Write it in third person. Neutral tone, not a tagline. Include what the product does, who it's for, and where to learn more.

2. **Product Description** — 1-2 paragraphs, written for a non-technical journalist. Benefit-led. Explains what the product does, why it exists, and what makes it different. No jargon. No mechanism-first explanations. The reader should understand the product after one read.

3. **Fact Sheet** — Bullet-point snapshot. Scannable in under 30 seconds. Include: founding date, platform(s), pricing, availability/regions, key stats (if verified), and any notable milestones. Each bullet is one clear fact. No sentences, no context — just the facts.

4. **Key Features** — 4-6 features, benefit-led. Each feature gets a name and one sentence explaining what it does for the user. Not "built with ML" — "recognizes your swing automatically, no manual tagging." Order by what matters most to the target press audience.

5. **Founder/Team Bios** — 50-100 words per person. Third person. Lead with credibility (relevant experience, past companies, domain expertise). End with a human detail if it's genuinely interesting. Include title and full name. One bio per person, one at a time.

6. **Visual Assets Checklist** — Don't generate image files, but produce a clear checklist of what the user needs to prepare: logo formats (SVG, PNG light/dark), app icon sizes, screenshots (current OS/device), lifestyle imagery, and any product video. Note any that are critical vs. nice-to-have.

7. **Sample Press Release** — A ready-to-send press release for the kit's purpose (launch, update, funding, evergreen). Standard format: headline, dateline, lead paragraph (who/what/when/where/why), supporting body paragraphs, a quote from the founder, a second quote from a customer or partner if available, boilerplate, and media contact block. Flag any sections where the user needs to supply a real quote.

8. **Press FAQ** — 5-7 questions a journalist is likely to ask, pre-answered. Prioritize questions about: what problem it solves, who it's for, what's different about it, pricing, availability, and what's coming next. Answers should be concise and quotable — a journalist can lift them directly.

9. **Media Contact Block** — Ready to paste. Format: Name, Title, Email, Phone (if provided), Website. Nothing else.

### PR Kit Rules

1. **Every stat must be confirmed.** Before including any number, ask the user to verify it. A wrong stat in a press kit damages credibility more than no stat.
2. **Write for the journalist, not the customer.** Press kit copy is neutral and informative, not persuasive. The CMO will push for stronger language — the PR Director must hold the line on press-document tone.
3. **Third person throughout.** Boilerplate, bios, press release body — all third person. The only exception is direct quotes.
4. **No jargon without a plain-English explanation.** If a technical term must appear, follow it with what it means.
5. **Availability must be accurate.** Do not write "available worldwide" unless it is. Do not list a launch date without confirming it with the user.
6. **Flag missing assets.** If the user hasn't mentioned visuals, remind them during the Visual Assets Checklist component. A press kit without logos is incomplete.
7. **Boilerplate is reused everywhere.** The Company Boilerplate written in step 1 should appear verbatim at the bottom of the press release in step 7. Don't rewrite it.
8. **One component at a time.** Propose the full draft for each component. Get explicit approval or edits. Finalize. Then and only then move to the next component.

### PR Kit Checklist (end of session)

After all components are approved, present a final checklist:

- [ ] Company Boilerplate
- [ ] Product Description
- [ ] Fact Sheet
- [ ] Key Features
- [ ] Founder/Team Bios
- [ ] Visual Assets (checklist provided — assets to be prepared by user)
- [ ] Sample Press Release
- [ ] Press FAQ
- [ ] Media Contact Block

Note any components that need follow-up (unconfirmed stats, missing quotes, assets not yet prepared).

## App Preview Mode

### Overview

Plans an App Store preview video (15-30s). Produces a production-ready plan: target devices, exact dimensions, poster frame choice, a beat-by-beat outline with timing and captions, audio direction, and a pre-flight checklist. Does NOT produce video files.

Before starting, capture:
- **Target devices.** Which device classes get their own preview (6.9" iPhone, 13" iPad, Mac, TV, Vision Pro)
- **Locales.** Primary plus any localized variants
- **What the app is.** One-sentence positioning, top 3 features to showcase, the one outcome that defines success for the user
- **Recording setup.** Simulator, device, or screen-capture tool the user will use

### The App Preview Panel

**GTM (Go-to-Market):** Accuracy and App Review compliance. Flags anything that would get the preview rejected (fake notifications, copyrighted music, third-party content, pricing claims). Confirms every feature shown is in the shipping build.

**CMO (Chief Marketing Officer):** Positioning and brand. Owns whether the first 2 seconds answer "what is this?" with the strongest possible hook. Pushes for the boldest beat order.

**Motion Director:** Craft for silent-autoplay. Owns pacing (2-3s per beat), caption readability at thumbnail scale, poster-frame selection, and the silent-first rule (no story that requires audio). Calls out logo intros, fades, and slow openers.

### The Core Narrative (the difference between great and mediocre)

Great previews have a story arc. Mediocre ones screen-record a demo walkthrough and call it done.

The arc has three acts:

1. **Beginning. The problem.** Show the friction, the mess, the gap. The viewer should recognize their own situation in 1-2 seconds.
2. **Middle. The app solving it.** Real UI, real interaction, the actual fix. Two or three feature beats that ladder up to the solution.
3. **End. The punch.** What your life looks like using it. Outcome, payoff, the emotional after-state. Not "here is the dashboard," but "here is what the dashboard gives you."

Every beat decision should ladder back to this arc. If a feature beat doesn't advance problem-to-solution-to-outcome, cut it.

### Beat-by-Beat Implementation (~22-25 seconds)

The three acts cash out as five beats with timing:

1. **0-2s. Problem hook.** Show the friction or the moment the app exists to fix. One-line caption names it. No logos, no splash, no "Presenting..." Skip the intro entirely.
2. **3-8s. Core solution.** The single action your app exists to do, performed live. Caption frames it as the benefit, not the feature.
3. **9-16s. Feature beats.** Two or three secondary features at roughly 3 seconds each. One idea per beat, one caption per beat. Hard cuts, no fades. Every beat reinforces the solution.
4. **17-22s. The punch.** Outcome, before/after, in-UI social proof, streak, achievement, the result on screen. This is "what your life looks like."
5. **23-25s. Soft close.** App name plus tagline overlay. No price, no "Download now" CTA. The Get button is right next to the video.

Aim for 20-25s total. 30s is the ceiling, not the target. Shorter completes more often.

### App Preview Specs (Apple, current 2026)

**Duration:** 15s minimum, 30s maximum.

**File:** Max 500 MB. Codecs: H.264 (10-12 Mbps VBR, up to High Profile Level 4.0) or ProRes 422 HQ (~220 Mbps, .mov only). Frame rate 30 fps max. Audio AAC 256 kbps stereo (H.264) or PCM 16-32 bit (ProRes).

**Per-locale limit:** Up to 3 previews per locale. Only the first preview autoplays in search results, muted, landscape.

**Poster frame:** Defaults to the 5-second mark. Override to a hand-picked frame in App Store Connect. Treat it like a still hero image, since users with autoplay off only see this.

**Device dimensions:**

| Device | Resolution (portrait / landscape) |
|---|---|
| iPhone 6.9" / 6.5" / 6.3" / 6.1" | 886 x 1920 / 1920 x 886 |
| iPhone 5.5" | 1080 x 1920 / 1920 x 1080 |
| iPhone 4.7" | 750 x 1334 / 1334 x 750 |
| iPad 13" / 12.9" / 11" | 1200 x 1600 / 1600 x 1200 |
| Mac (landscape only) | 1920 x 1080 |
| Apple TV (landscape only) | 1920 x 1080 |
| Apple Vision Pro (landscape only) | 3840 x 2160 |

Coverage rule: one 6.9" iPhone preview plus one 13" iPad preview covers every modern iOS device.

**Export pattern that always passes:** H.264, 10 Mbps VBR, 30 fps, 886 x 1920, AAC 256 kbps stereo, .mp4.

### App Review Hard Rules (instant rejection)

- Screen captures of YOUR own app only. No third-party content, no marketing-website screens.
- No pricing, availability, or promotional claims ("Free!", "50% off").
- No Apple emoji.
- No copyrighted music for entertainment value. iTunes/Apple Music tracks are only allowed if you link as a music preview.
- No fake iOS UI (fake notifications, fake share sheets, fake Siri).
- No real user data. Use fictional accounts and names.
- No content above a 4+ rating, regardless of the app's actual rating.
- Third-party device frames around your UI risk rejection. Apple expects clean screen captures with overlays.

### Transitions: Brand Cards as Bridges

Strong previews alternate live app UI with full-bleed **brand cards** (a single saturated color from the app's palette, holding one short caption). The app UI slides off to reveal the next brand card underneath, so the transition itself is a brand moment. No cuts, no fades, no third-party transition effects.

The brand cards do four jobs at once:
- **Pace control.** A 1-2 second beat of solid color resets the eye between dense UI screens.
- **Caption surface.** The marketing message lives on the brand card, never overlaid on UI chrome. No fight between message and product.
- **Brand reinforcement.** The brand color is the stage between every feature beat.
- **Wipe device.** The app UI literally slides out to reveal the brand color, so the transition is the brand showing itself.

When to use it: app UI is visually dense (charts, lists, dashboards) or there are 3+ feature beats. Skip for games or single-flow apps where one continuous take is stronger.

**Apply per-app, not as a template.** The principle is brand-card-as-bridge; the styling is yours.

| Variable | What to decide for this specific app |
|---|---|
| Brand color | The single most-saturated color in the app's identity. Not orange unless the app is orange. |
| Caption typography | The app's own type system. Same weight and tracking it uses inside the product. |
| Caption count | 3-5 captions across the preview, one per major feature beat. |
| Caption length | 2-4 words. Verb-led or noun-promise. Match the app's voice. |
| Card duration | 1-2 seconds. Long enough to read, short enough to feel like a beat. |
| Transition direction | Slide up, slide down, slide sideways, or scale — whichever matches the app's existing motion language. Pick one and reuse it. |
| First card | If the brand is well-known, lead with the wordmark. If unknown, lead with the strongest benefit caption and earn the wordmark later. |

Pick one brand color, one typeface, one transition direction, and reuse them everywhere in the video. Variance kills the effect.

(Strava's preview is one expression of this pattern: orange, custom display type, vertical slide. Your app's expression should look like your app, not like Strava.)

### Learnings to Lift from Any Reference Preview

When a user shares a preview they admire, do not copy its surface choices. Extract the underlying moves, then re-cast them in the user's brand and product. The transferable moves usually include:

- **Where the hook lands.** Strava opens on the payoff (Course Record + confetti), not the splash. Identify which moment in the reference IS the hook, then find the analogous payoff moment in the user's app.
- **Cadence between brand cards and UI.** Count the seconds. Is it 1s card / 3s UI / 1s card / 3s UI, or something else? Match the rhythm, not the visuals.
- **Caption diction.** Count syllables. Note verb-led vs. noun-led. Note whether captions name the feature or the benefit. Mirror the diction with the user's own copy.
- **Transition device.** Slide, scale, mask reveal, match-cut on motion. Pick one that fits the user's app and reuse it.
- **Who appears on camera.** Strava chose ordinary people, not athletes. Decide who shows up in the user's preview based on the same logic, the answer is rarely "elite users."
- **What is NOT shown.** Reference previews are often as strong for what they cut as for what they include. Note any obvious feature the reference left out and ask whether the user's preview can make the same trade.

Run the panel against these extracted moves, not against the reference's styling.

### Captioning Craft

- One caption per beat, 3-5 words, verb-led.
- On a brand card if you're using the Strava intercut pattern. Otherwise upper third or lower third so it does not fight UI chrome.
- Type size roughly 64-80pt at 1920x886. Must be readable at thumbnail size.
- High-contrast pill or shadow behind text when overlaid on UI. App Store backgrounds vary by light/dark mode.
- Hold each caption 2.5-3 seconds. Faster and people cannot read it.

### Pacing

- Cuts every 2-3 seconds. Never linger over 3s on a static screen.
- Match-cut motion across cuts (a swipe in beat 2 finishes in beat 3) keeps it kinetic without feeling jumpy.

### Common Mistakes (the don't list)

1. Demo walkthroughs with no arc. The mediocre default. Tour of features with no problem named and no payoff shown.
2. Logo intros or splash screens that burn the hook window.
3. Slow fade-ins. First frame should already be in-app and visually loud.
4. Narration that requires audio. If muted viewers cannot follow it, the video has failed.
5. Tiny captions that vanish at thumbnail size.
6. Third-party device frames that crop or cover real UI.
7. Fake notifications, fake Siri, fake iMessage popups. Auto-rejected.
8. Showing marketing-website screens instead of the app.
9. Music as the spine. Story must work silent.
10. Trying to show every feature. Three features done clearly beats eight in a blur.
11. A poster frame that is a logo. Make it a UI hero shot with a value caption.

### App Preview Process

1. **Capture context** (devices, locales, features, recording tool, the single outcome).
2. **Lock the arc.** Confirm the problem, the solution, and the punch before any beat work. If the user cannot name the problem in one sentence, the preview is not ready to plan.
3. **Run the panel** on the 5-beat outline. Present beat list with timing and captions. Get approval.
4. **Pick the poster frame.** Identify which moment in the outline doubles as the autoplay-off hero. Confirm with the user.
5. **Audio direction.** Music/SFX/narration choice plus a confirmation it is licensed or original. Confirm the story works silent.
6. **Localization plan.** Identify which locales get their own recording vs. caption-only swap.
7. **Pre-flight checklist.** Output a final checklist before the user records or hands the plan to an editor.

### Pre-Flight Checklist (end of session)

- [ ] Arc is clear: problem named, solution shown, punch delivered
- [ ] Duration between 15s and 30s (target 20-25s)
- [ ] Resolution matches target device class (e.g., 886 x 1920 for iPhone)
- [ ] H.264 at 10-12 Mbps VBR, 30 fps, AAC 256 kbps stereo
- [ ] Poster frame selected (not a logo, not the default 5s unless it works)
- [ ] All beats use real app UI (no fake notifications/Siri, no marketing-site screens)
- [ ] All captions readable at thumbnail size
- [ ] No prices, no Apple emoji, no copyrighted music
- [ ] Story works with audio muted
- [ ] Localized variants planned for top markets

## App Screenshots Mode

### Overview

Plans App Store screenshots (up to 10 static images per device class per locale). Produces a production-ready plan with shot-by-shot roles, captions, layout choice, and localization strategy. Does NOT produce image files.

Before starting, capture:
- **Target devices.** 6.9" iPhone, 13" iPad, Mac, TV, Vision Pro, Watch as applicable
- **Locales.** Primary plus any localized variants
- **Style choice.** Marketing (framed + headline + background), raw (pure app UI), or hybrid (top-third headline, bottom device frame)
- **Top 5 features and the single outcome** to lead with

### The App Screenshots Panel

**GTM (Go-to-Market):** Accuracy and App Review compliance. Flags pricing on screenshots, competitor names, fake awards/stars, App Store ranking claims, non-Apple device frames. Verifies any stat or feature shown is real.

**CMO (Chief Marketing Officer):** Positioning and the first-three priority. Owns whether shots 1-3 (visible in search results without tapping in) answer "what is this?" and "why install?" in two seconds. Pushes for the boldest hero shot.

**Visual Designer:** Hierarchy, typography, thumbnail readability. Owns headline length, contrast against light/dark App Store backgrounds, color consistency across all 10 shots, text-expansion headroom for localized variants.

### The Core Narrative (same arc, different surface)

Screenshots tell the same problem-solution-punch story as previews, stretched across 10 stills.

- **Shots 1-3: Problem and promise.** Hero answers "what is this?" Shot 2 names the core solution. Shot 3 reinforces with proof.
- **Shots 4-8: The solution at work.** Feature beats, each one a concrete action and benefit.
- **Shots 9-10: The punch.** Outcome and lifestyle. What your life looks like using it. Not another feature.

Mediocre screenshot sets are 10 feature shots in a row. Strong sets have a beginning, middle, and end.

### The First 3 Rule

The first 3 portrait screenshots (or 1-2 landscape) render in search results before users tap into the listing. They do roughly 80% of the conversion work. Lock these first, run the panel on them as their own pass, then plan the remaining seven.

### The 10-Shot Arc

| # | Role | Example caption |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Hero / problem-promise | "Your golf round, scored" |
| 2 | Core solution | "Auto-track every shot" |
| 3 | Trust / proof | "Featured by Apple, 4.9 stars" |
| 4 | Feature beat A | "GPS distances on every hole" |
| 5 | Feature beat B | "Live scorecard sync" |
| 6 | Feature beat C | "Share rounds in one tap" |
| 7 | Differentiator | "Works offline anywhere" |
| 8 | Platform extension | "And on your Watch" |
| 9 | Outcome / lifestyle | A real round being played |
| 10 | Punch / soft CTA | "Start your first round free" |

### App Screenshots Specs (Apple, current 2026)

**Formats:** .png, .jpg, .jpeg. RGB only (sRGB or Display P3). 72 dpi. **No alpha channel** — flatten before export (PNGs with transparency are rejected).

**Quantity:** Minimum 1, maximum 10 per device class per locale. Always fill all 10 slots.

**iPhone (portrait):**

| Display | Required? | Pixel dimensions | Devices |
|---|---|---|---|
| 6.9" | Yes (preferred) | 1320 x 2868 (also 1290 x 2796) | iPhone Air, 17 Pro Max, 16 Pro Max, 15 Pro Max, 14 Pro Max |
| 6.5" | Only if 6.9" not provided | 1284 x 2778 (also 1242 x 2688) | iPhone 14 Plus, 13 Pro Max, 11 Pro Max |
| 6.3" | Optional (auto-scaled from 6.9") | 1206 x 2622 (also 1179 x 2556) | iPhone 17 Pro, 16 Pro, 15 Pro |
| 5.5" | No longer required | 1242 x 2208 | iPhone 8 Plus (legacy only) |

**iPad (portrait):**

| Display | Required? | Pixel dimensions |
|---|---|---|
| 13" | Required if app runs on iPad | 2064 x 2752 (also 2048 x 2732) |
| 12.9" / 11" | Optional | 2048 x 2732 / 1488 x 2266 |

**Mac:** 16:10 aspect. Accepted: 1280 x 800, 1440 x 900, 2560 x 1600, 2880 x 1800. Upload the largest you can render cleanly.

**Apple TV:** 1920 x 1080 or 3840 x 2160.

**Apple Vision Pro:** 3840 x 2160.

**Apple Watch:** Required for Watch apps. Use the same screenshot dimensions across ALL localizations (Apple enforces this). Series 11/10: 416 x 496. Ultra 3: 422 x 514. Series 9/8/7: 396 x 484. **No device frame allowed** in Watch screenshots, UI only.

**Inheritance:** Apple auto-scales DOWN within a family. 6.9" iPhone fills in for 6.5", 6.3", 6.1", 5.5", 4.7". 13" iPad fills in for 12.9", 11", 10.5", 9.7". Minimum viable upload for a universal iOS app: 6.9" iPhone + 13" iPad, plus Mac/TV/Vision/Watch if those targets exist.

### App Review Hard Rules (instant rejection)

- No pricing on screenshots ("$0.99", "Free", "50% off"). Prices vary by region.
- No competitor names or logos.
- No Apple-device frames around non-Apple-device UI (e.g., your Android screen inside an iPhone frame).
- No fake awards, fake reviews, fake star ratings, fabricated press quotes.
- No claims about App Store ranking ("#1 in Productivity").
- Apple Watch screenshots: no device frame at all.
- Standard content policy applies (no violence, sexual content, misleading claims).

### Caption Craft

- 3-6 words max. Verb plus benefit plus result.
- Good: "Plan your week in 30 seconds." Bad: "Productivity app with calendar features."
- Lead with the user's outcome, not the feature name.
- Readable at thumbnail size. If you cannot read it on a 200px-wide preview, it is too small.
- High-contrast (white on saturated color, or black on white). Mid-tone gradients vanish at small sizes.

### Marketing vs Raw vs Hybrid

- **Marketing** (framed + headline + background): higher CTR for productivity, utility, lifestyle apps. Required if your raw UI is dense or unfamiliar.
- **Raw** (pure app UI, full-bleed): better for games or visually stunning apps where the UI is the sell.
- **Hybrid** (top-third headline + background, bottom two-thirds device frame with real UI): the format roughly 70% of top-grossing apps use. Default to this unless there is a clear reason not to.

### Portrait vs Landscape

- Portrait is the default and what search results render.
- Landscape screenshots occupy 2 slots in search results, so one landscape image dominates. Useful for games or wide experiences.
- Do not mix orientations within a listing.

### Color and Dark Mode

- App Store renders listings in both light and dark mode. Test screenshots against both.
- Pure white or pure black edges bleed into page chrome. Use slight padding or a colored backdrop.
- Keep brand color consistent across all 10 shots. Signals a coherent product.

### Localization Gotchas

- **Text expansion.** German and Russian expand 30-40% over English. Finnish similar. Design hero-headline space with headroom.
- **RTL (Arabic, Hebrew).** Mirror the entire layout, not just the text. Device frames flip, icon placement flips.
- **CJK (zh, ja, ko).** Default Latin fonts will not render properly. Use SF Pro or Noto Sans with broad Unicode coverage. Korean and Japanese need different line-height tuning than Latin.
- **Cleanest pipeline:** localize the app UI itself, retake raw screenshots in each language, overlay localized headlines. Keep background and headline as separate Figma layers from the device frame.

### Tooling

- **Figma + plugins** (Screeny, AppMockUp AI plugin): best for hybrid layouts. Where most teams converge.
- **AppMockUp, Previewed, Screenshot.rocks, Picstack, Shots, Rotato:** browser-based or Mac-native generators. Fast for v1.
- **Fastlane snapshot + frameit:** automates raw capture across every device/language combo from XCUITests. Best for 10+ language teams.
- **`asc-shots-pipeline` skill** in this repo: end-to-end automated capture, frame, and upload using xcodebuild/simctl, AXe, and `asc screenshots`. Recommend when the user is shipping to App Store Connect.

### App Screenshots Process

1. **Capture context** (devices, locales, style, top features, the outcome).
2. **Lock the arc.** Confirm problem-solution-punch maps to shot ranges before naming individual shots.
3. **Run the panel on shots 1-3** as a focused pass. These do most of the conversion work.
4. **Run the panel on shots 4-10** for the full arc.
5. **Decide framing.** Marketing, raw, or hybrid. Confirm with user.
6. **Localization plan.** Which locales get their own captions; whether the app UI needs to be re-recorded in those languages.
7. **Tooling handoff.** Recommend Figma + plugin, Fastlane snapshot + frameit, or `asc-shots-pipeline` depending on team and locale count.
8. **Pre-flight checklist.** Output a final checklist before design or upload.

### Pre-Flight Checklist (end of session)

- [ ] Arc is clear: problem named in shots 1-3, solution in 4-8, punch in 9-10
- [ ] 6.9" iPhone uploaded at 1320 x 2868 PNG, no alpha, sRGB or P3
- [ ] 13" iPad uploaded at 2064 x 2752 if app runs on iPad
- [ ] Mac at 2880 x 1800, TV at 3840 x 2160, Vision at 3840 x 2160, Watch sized correctly if applicable
- [ ] All 10 slots filled
- [ ] First 3 shots tested at thumbnail size, headlines still readable
- [ ] No prices, no competitor names, no fake awards/stars, no non-Apple device frames
- [ ] Status bar shows 9:41, full battery, full signal
- [ ] Localized variants for primary plus top 3 non-English markets
- [ ] Headlines tested for German/Russian text expansion if those locales ship
- [ ] Screenshots render correctly against App Store light and dark mode
